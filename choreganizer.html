Choreganizer

Role

Collaborators

Sushant Gupta
Ngoc Huynh
Tenzin Tsundae

Duration

10 Weeks

Project Timeline

Discovering a Problem

We explored the environment around us and discovered a commonality among our group members and audience, and that is most of us are college students and share a living space with others. After noticing that college students experience roommate struggles related to cleanliness, we developed our problem statement: College student households may find it difficult to have consistency in the completion of chores around the house due to the neglect of chores and lack of responsibility; ultimately, leading to stress and discord among roommates.

Competitive Analysis

When we had recognized our problem we wanted to solved, we explored how others have tried to solve it before. We found some consistencies and features that we believed needed to be emphasized. I had noticed a lot of desirable features in the Spotless app while finding that it lacked some basic customization that would improve the user experience.

Storyboard

To further understand how this application could be useful in a college student household, I created a storyboard that highlights a common roommate concern of one pulling more than their own weight with chores, and how the Choreganizer can help distribute house tasks more evenly.

User Insights

Our team created a user survey to understand the current living and housemate situations of college students, and it was distributed among our peers. The user survey provided insight on the communication levels and current chore assigning methods of the students, which then led to the students providing their suggestions and favor toward particular features. The scale was from 1 to 5, 1 being "strongly disagree" and 5 being "strongly agree."

The apparent takeaway from these survey results is the lack of communication and understanding of responsibilities in regards to chores amongst roommates. Our team thus made improving communication a priority goal for our product.

Personas

We further explored the benefits of multiple possible features for the Choreganizer by applying them to two personas that were heavily informed by the insight from the user survey. My teammate produced the personas of Lloyd and Jordan to get an understanding of Choreganizerâ€™s difference in usage. Both have their respective struggles with communication, Lloyd having little experience livng with others and Jordan being a busy body, and both would find Choreganizer as the solution to bridge that gap.

Sketches

Initially we sketched some low fidelity mock-ups of possible arrangements of our proposed features. This was my contribution to the low fidelity brainstorming. The left image was produced during a brainstorm exercise. The right image was a low fidelity sketch produced after having reviewed our entire teams sketches and agreeing on interface choices.

We had gathered from our data that the most desired of our proposed features are notifications & reminders, chore descriptions, chore transfer, zones, to-do list, chore order, and transparent chore assignment. Focusing on the features we want to implement, we consolidated aspect from all of our sketches and created a new low fidelity sketch to reach a presentation of the features as desired to elevate the user experience.

Prototype

We created a prototype that reflects the interface design that we outlined in our sketches. We also chose a color palette that gave a warm feeling and reflected our team name The Aldrich Wanderers.

The notable aspects of our design are the to-do lists of each roommate and chore assignment features. The user can view their own chore to-do list and view their housematesâ€™ as well which makes the collective completion of chores a collaborative effort and shared responsibility. The chore assignment system also promotes a collaborative process by allowing any member of the household to create a new task and assign it to another housemate and allow it to cycle through the roommate chore assignment order or set it as a one-time task.
